How it works

Two layers. Neither one moves an employee.

Layer one

Your freelance bench, pooled

You are not trading your network for someone else's. You are trading one network for a thousand.

You might not get the exact person you had in mind, but the odds that you get nobody collapse. And your bench is bigger than you think: the freelancers you use, the hire who left last year, the intern who finished in June.

Layer two

Your team, skill-mapped

You do not need spare people. You need twenty minutes from someone who has already solved it.

Teams here are small. Nobody has staff to lend, which is exactly why the usual version of this idea dies on contact with this building.

The mechanism, and its problem

Credits, not cash. And here is what is wrong with them.

Time banks reliably collapse. The research names three reasons. I have answers to two.

01

Double coincidence of wants

You need someone who needs what you have, at the same moment. In a small pool that match is not there, and the system stalls before it starts.

✓ Answered by density
02

Credit imbalance

Some firms consume far more than they give. Their balance hits zero, they get locked out, they leave. The heavy givers subsidise everyone and resent it.

✓ Answered by earning
03

No price signal

Credits have no exchange rate. Nothing says whether twenty minutes with a senior ML engineer costs what twenty minutes with a junior designer costs.
